NO-GI BJJ

Ensnare Effortlessly

As a no-Gi BJJ practitioner, I find that this style of jiu-jitsu is heavily reliant on understanding the fundamentals of human movement and physiology. This is not to say that Gi-based BJJ does not require the same high level of understanding, I assure you that I does. I'm simply pointing out what is obvious, that no-Gi BJJ does away with the Gi, the historical and quintessential fixure of the martial art itself.

I need to interpret and react to my opponent's body movement in a entirely different way than when the Gi is present. The Gi was my suit of armor, my sword, and my shield. Lacking all the friction, drag, and grips that the Gi provides, I have to focus on tracking and controling my opponent in a completely different way. Without the Gi, I now have deal with an opponent that can move a lot less restricted than before, with less attacking/defending options at my disposal.

There exists a vast number of ways to address the differences between the two branches of BJJ. Here, I have selected the three components that I focus on to improve my no-Gi game.

Movement

Lacking Gi grips, I must use opponent's natural body handles to control their movement. My opponents neck, elbows, wrists, hips, knees, and ankles will provide these handles for me.

No-gi BJJ requires the mastery of overhooks and underhooks. It's important to know when to use them and how to defend against them to control posture and movement.

Strategy

As a match increases in duration, sweat becomes an increasingly important factor. Reduced friction and drag make it is easier to slip and escape out of unfavorable positions. Conversely, it is harder to keep an opponent down for too long. Anticipating my opponents sweeps/escapes will keep me one step ahead.

A faster pace combined with a more mobile opponent reduces the time I have to chain complex movement patterns together. I keep moving in order to "catch" my opponent in transition to secure a submission.

Technique

Without grips on my opponent, some techniques are cannot be executed the way they were intended to with the Gi. I rely on catching and using my opoonents natural handles to apply pressure and execute techniques.

Since it is tough to slow down a slick and fast opponent, I have to focus on overhooks and underhooks to control their positioning and limit their movement. I rely on executing simple techniques rather than orchastrating elaborate, multi-step ones.

Stand-up Concepts

Before I can flow with my Jiu-Jitsu ground game, I need a solid stand-up game. With no-gi, I prefer to manage space and shoot for leg takedowns. I try to avoid engaging in a clinch-fight. I use constant level-changes and vary my movement speed to expose their legs for the takedown shot.

Lacking, gi grips, It is a little harder for opponents to control me in a no-gi setting long enough to sucessfully pull guard. My main concerns are protecting my neck from guillotines and protecting my arms from a kimura grip counters when I shoot for leg takedowns.

Clinch-control

In no-gi BJJ, grip-fighting is less important. Most takedowns and scrambles for positioning are won and lost by who has a better use of under/overhooks. Swimming and establishing solid clinch-control depends on how effective my hooks are in breaking down my oponents posture. For this reason, controlling what happens in the clinch is important.

Takedowns

If I engage in a clinch, I keep a good base and swim my arms and wrists to control my opponents and establish good under/over hooks. I find it useful to keep my posture low so I can sprawl to stuff their takedown or shoot for a leg takedown of my own. The hips are the main source of power in BJJ and the centerpiece to effective takedowns. I want my hips free and their's under control.

Guard-play Concepts

My Guard is all about controlling my opponent. From my guard, I can sweep, submit, and restrict an opponent. The guard is considered an advantageous position, since several attack options are readily available at almost no risk. Without the Gi, I skip the grip-fighting and work to prevent my opponent from initiating a guard-pass by controlling their bodies natural handles. I use underhooks, overhooks, and swim my arms and wrists to effectively breaking down their posture. This helps me ensure I am in a position of control when I begin my attacks/sweeps.

Full-Guard

Normally refered to as a "closed guard", I completely trap my opponent inside my crossed legs. Lacking grips, I find it best for me to sweep and take mount instead of forcing an arm exposure. The key for me to attack from my full guard is to keep my oponents posture broken and off balance. Here are some popular variations of the full guard:
High-guard
Rubber-guard

Half-Guard

The half-Guard requires me to entangle and trap one of my opponents legs. The key to a successful half-guard is to be able to lay on my side and move my hips out effectively rather than laying flat on my back. From a good half guard, I seek to sweep to a better position or transition to full guard. Here are some popular variations of the half-guard:
Lock-down
Deep half-guard
Z-guard
X-guard

Open-Guard

The open guard is designed for keeping an opponent under control while in transition between different positions, it is not a static guard. There is a difference in choosing to play "open guard" vs. being forced to play "open guard". In the former, I purposefully open my guard to execute multiple sweeps or attacks. In the latter, your opponent forces me to scramble to recover my guard. Here are some popular variations of the open-guard:
Butterfy-guard
De la Riva/Reverse De la Riva
Inverted-Guard

Guard-Passing

One of the most important aspects of no-gi BJJ is guard-passing. Lacking the Gi, guard passing is much more fluid and dynamic. When I initiate a guard pass, I make sure I maintain good posture and a balanced base. I restrict my opponents movement by simultaneously controlling their hips/legs and apply structural pressure to their guard. When I'm in an opponents guard, I am cautious of exposing space for my opponents to use to initiate attacks/sweeps.s

Pass Full-Guard

Full-guard passes can be categorized into two types: "Prying" or "collapsing" the guard. "Prying" involves opening your opponents legs and preventing them from rewrapping you. "Collapsing" involves folding their legs together, preventing them from reopening as you pass. When passing, fill useful space your opponent gives up, or else you risk being set-up for a sweep or a submission.

Pass Half-Guard

I prevent my opponent from shooting to a deep half guard and flatten them on to their back, eliminating sweeping/attacking options. Most people use the "lockdown" half guard variation in no-gi, so I prefer to free my leg out before I pass. I lower myself into their hips and use my arms to control tie thier legs as I use my shoulder to control their hips. I use my feet to undo their lockdown and begin my pass.

Pass Open-Guard

Open guard passing requires active movement because of how dynamic the open guard can be. In no-gi, I have to be careful of letting my opponent trap and control my legs with their DLR/RDLR hooks. Not having to think about grips allwos me to focus on executing a leg-drag or arm-drag when my opponent tries to control my legs or hips. I proceed to expose openings in my opponents open guard so that I can go over, around, or underneath these exposures to complete my pass.

Dominating

Maintaining control in a dominant position is trickier than most people think, especially in no-gi BJJ. Traditional gi-based positions require grips in order to fully control your opponent. With no-gi, these position-dominating techniques must be done without grips, focusing more on managing open spaces to block an opponent's sweep/escape. I focus on controlling my opponent's body handles to disrupting your their structural defenses while maintaining my own composure as I obtain my submission.

The Mount

Arguably the most dominant position in bjj, maintaining control of the mount requires an understanding of the position. The mount is an unstable position, I need to be able to stay on top of an opponent that is desperately trying to create space to escape. I keep my hooks tight under my opponent, widen my knees and sink my hips onto them to lower my center of gravity so that I can "surf" my opponent's escape maneuvers. My hands are free to attack when my opponent makes a mistake. Variations to the mount:
Low-mount
Technical-mount
High mount

Side-Control

Since I don't have grips, I prefer the scarf-hold variation of the side-control position. I lay the weight of my body diagonally across their body, using my hips to block theirs. I use my underhooks to control their upperbody as I drive my weight onto them. I prevent my opponent from turning onto their side and keep them flat on their back so I can begin my attack.

North-South

In no-gi, I prefer this position only as a transition between opposide side-controls since I can't use the collar to attack a choke. I keep the position by holding my opponents hips, keeping my weight on their chest, and my clamping my elbows tight to their body tucked underneath their armpits to prevent them from turning and escaping.

Back Mount

In no-gi, keeping control of the back mount depends on my body positioning and my leg hooks. I don't have a collar to grip-fight over, so I clamp a tight "seat belt" with my arms to lock them my opponent in position. In no-gi, I try to turn my opponent onto their stomach so I can flatten them out. I ensure to keep my hips and shoulders above theirs. These techniques afford me control as I look for a submission.

Dominated Concepts

In my experience, escaping from an opponents dominant position comes down to three things:
1.) Recognizing where my opponents base is
2.) Recognizing how and what my opponent is attacking
3.) Recognizing how to create and close off space to set up my escape.
These three concepts will help me compromise my opponents balance, disrupt their attacks, and provide me the space to escape and repose my guard.

Escape The Mount

In no-gi, arm attacks are very common so I tuck my arms in tight to protect them from being isolated. Under my opponent's mount, I hip bump and turn sideways to reach and trap of my opponent's same-side leg hook. When the hook is trapped, I push on their knee and shrimp out to create space for my leg to establish a half-guard. I repeat the process to the other side to get a full-guard. If I can't undo a leg hook, I simply trap their same-side ankle and arm to bridge in that direction to sweep and end-up in their full-guard.

Escape Side-Control

The key to escape or sweep my opponent from their side-control is to not let them flatten me out. If I am flattened out, I secure a frame on their neck with my near-side arm to prevent it from being isolated. I simultaneously hip bump into them and push their neck away to give me the space I need to hip escape away and onto my side. I immediately swivel my hips and knee back underneath them and all the way to the other side into a modified butterfly-esque guard. I hip escape once more to lock in a full-guard to finish my escape.

Escape North-South

Most of my training partners sprawl out when they have me in N/S preventing me from framing on their hips, so I use a "side door" to escape the N/S position into their side control. I first protect my arms by tucking my arms tight against their body below their armpits. From underneath them, I reach a far-side arm to fram on their hips and walk myself into their side-control. From there, I simply perform my favorite side-control escape/sweep.

Escape Back Mount

Since there is no gi, I don't have to defend any collar-chokes and can focus on defending the most common no-gi back mount attack, the RNC. Most opponents "seat belt" me to restrict my movement. To escape, I defend my neck by tucking my chin in towards the elbow that is around my neck and drop on the side of the underhooked arm. This entire time, I have both my hands securing my opponent's top arm and preventing it from locking in the choke. As we fall, I use my top foot to release the bottom hook as I shift my hips out to turn and face them. I finish my escape by traping their top leg between my own to get into a modified half-guard.
